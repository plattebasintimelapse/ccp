By Steven Speicher
April 17, 2015

Elm Creek Complex

Sandhill cranes gather to roost on a sandbar in the Platte River at the Elm Creek Complex. (Michael Forsberg)

About 15 miles west of Kearney is the Elm Creek Complex, southeast of Elm Creek. The complex encompasses the Kearney Canal Diversion, a chokepoint on the river just below the Elm Creek bridge.

The nature of the irrigation canal diversion consolidates the river into a single channel, which can be negative for wildlife. As a result, conservation efforts have focused on creating wider channels that approximate the river width prior to 1930.

Beginning with Nebraska Public Power District (NPPD), habitat conservation efforts have spanned decades and organizations.

In the 1980s, the Crane Trust and The Nature Conservancy worked with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service on efforts like clearing trees and controlling invasive species. In 2010, they restored sloughs (old river channels or backwaters) in the cleared area.

Today, the Platte River Recovery Implementation Program (PRRIP) owns and manages the Elm Creek Complex. Their work includes herbicide spraying of invasive vegetation like phragmites in the channel and other riparian areas, removing brush and invasive woody vegetation from islands and grassland areas, clearing trees from the channel banks to improve open sight distance and encourage channel widening, and constructing in-channel nesting islands for least terns and piping plovers.

The Elm Creek Complex is a large stretch of managed area along the central Platte River Valley, spanning nearly four miles. The combination of NPPD's off-channel sandpit nesting habitat and their own constructed in-channel sandbars allows PRRIP to evaluate what kind of habitat least terns and piping plovers prefer. These studies can help prioritize future efforts.

Large-scale, collaborative habitat complexes like Elm Creek exemplify modern conservation work on the central Platte River.
